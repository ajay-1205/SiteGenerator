> George R.R. Martin did not directly copy J.R.R. Tolkien, but he was heavily **inspired** by him

![Martin with tolkein](/images/martin_tolkein.png)

George R.R. Martin viewing his own work as a more realistic, "darker" subversion of the high-fantasy tropes Tolkien established. While acknowledging Tolkien as a massive influence, Martin focuses on gritty political realism, flawed characters, and moral ambiguity, contrasted with Tolkien’s mythic, good-versus-evil world.


## Influence and Inspiration

Martin has stated that The Lord of the Rings had a profound impact on him as a child, and he re-reads it every few years.

## Subversion vs. Copying

Rather than replicating Tolkien, Martin intentionally sought to twist fantasy tropes, such as questioning the **heroic** nature of Aragorn's rule or focusing on the mundane consequences of war, often referred to as "subverting expectations".

## Tone Differences

Tolkien’s work is often seen as a romantic, epic myth, whereas Martin’s is characterized as "gutter fantasy" with a strong emphasis on complex human motivations, sex, and violence.

## Mythopoeia 

While Tolkien spent decades creating intricate languages, histories, and myths for his world, Martin focuses more on the political machinations and human interactions. 

## Notable Commonalities (Homages)

> Despite the differences, Martin includes specific nods to Tolkien

###### The Name: 

Born George Raymond Martin, he added the second "R" (for Richard) as a confirmation name at age 13, though many see the "G.R.R." as a branding homage to "J.R.R.".

###### Samwell Tarly:

Martin has acknowledged that Samwell Tarly is an homage to Samwise Gamgee.

###### Naming Conventions: 

Names like Oakenshield (a castle on the Wall) and characters like Beren Tallhart are direct references to Tolkien's lore.

## Conclusion

George R.R. Martin did not copy J.R.R. Tolkien, though he has openly stated that Tolkien’s work had an enormous influence on him. Rather than imitating the story, Martin wrote A Song of Ice and Fire (ASOIAF) as a deliberate response and deconstruction of the high-fantasy tropes Tolkien established. 

#### Key Distinctions and Philosophical Differences

While both authors created vast fictional worlds with deep lore, their approaches are fundamentally different

###### Mythology vs. History: 

Tolkien was a linguist and mythmaker whose goal was to create a "mythology for England," resulting in a world of symbolic nobility and cosmic justice. Martin, a fan of historical fiction, treats his world like a first-person account of history, focusing on political realism and human flaws.

###### Moral Ambiguity: 

Tolkien’s world often features a clear distinction between good and evil (e.g., Sauron as an external dark force). Martin’s work is famously "grey," arguing that the real struggle between good and evil takes place within the individual human heart.
